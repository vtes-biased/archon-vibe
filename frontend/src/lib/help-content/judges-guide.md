# Tournament Conduct & Infraction Guide

## Introduction

This guide establishes how judges should respond to rules violations in VTES tournaments, safeguarding fair play and competitive integrity.

* Section 1: general philosophy, definitions, and enforcement principles.
* Sections 2–4: infraction categories — Procedural Errors, Tournament Errors, and Unsportsmanlike Conduct — each further subdivided by issue type.
* Section 5: practical organization of running a tournament.

For terminology, consult the VTES Tournament Rules. In case of translation discrepancies, the English version is authoritative.

## 1. General Philosophy

Judges are impartial overseers who maintain order, ensure fair competition, and apply rules consistently. They intervene when a player requests help, a rules breach is observed, or a situation risks disrupting the event — but are not expected to prevent every mistake. They respond to issues as they occur, correct them, and communicate clearly. Proactive intervention is appropriate outside active play (registration, deck checks, card legality).

Penalties are corrective and preventative — not punitive. The judge's first responsibility is to help the player understand the violation; the penalty reinforces the lesson and deters repeat offenses.

Minor errors resolved fairly by the players themselves need no judge involvement. If the game state is clear but communication is confusing, the judge should seek clarification rather than assign a penalty. Significant problems must be handled through the appropriate infraction category.

Only the Head Judge may diverge from this guide, and only in extraordinary circumstances with no applicable philosophy. Routine factors (round, player skill or age, leniency, judge experience) do not justify deviation. Any judge considering an exception must consult the Head Judge first.

Judges are not exempt from error. Mistakes should be acknowledged and corrected when possible. If a player violated a rule based on flawed staff information, the Head Judge may reduce the penalty or authorize a roll-back, provided it is practical and does not create further imbalance.

### 1.1. Definition of Penalties

All formally tracked penalties (Warning and above) must be clearly communicated to the player, reported to the Head Judge, and submitted to the V:EKN Penalty Database. If the resolution takes more than a minute, a time extension may be granted (see [1.2.4 - Guidelines for Applying Time Extensions](#124-guidelines-for-applying-time-extensions)).

#### 1.1.1. Caution

A **Caution** is a verbal-only penalty for very minor, immediately fixable infractions that cause no lasting disruption and create no strategic advantage (e.g., playing the Chantry without a ready Tremere and not yet replacing it). The judge must explain the violation, correct procedure, and consequences of repetition. Cautions are not formally tracked, but the floor judge should inform the Head Judge to notice patterns.

#### 1.1.2. Warning

A **Warning** is a formally tracked penalty for incorrect play or procedural problems requiring time to fix (e.g., insufficient shuffling after a library search), or when a minor issue has escalated to affect the game state. It documents the infraction, notifies relevant parties, and discourages repeat offenses.

#### 1.1.3. Standings Adjustment

A **Standings Adjustment** is a severe penalty that imposes a **–1 VP** scoring consequence instead of altering the game state, affecting standings while preserving multiplayer integrity.

##### When the –1 VP Applies

* **During an ongoing game**: applies to that game.
* **Between preliminary rounds, or before round 1**: applies to the next game.
* **After the last preliminary round**: applies to the previous game.

##### Impact on GW and TP

A Standings Adjustment does not directly change GWs or TPs, but since a GW goes to the player with the highest VP at the table, the –1 VP may shift who receives the GW, and TPs adjust accordingly.

> **Example 1:** A player wins 3–2 and receives –1 VP → final VP is 2. If this ties for highest VP, the result becomes 2–2 (no GW). TPs follow the Tournament Rules for a 2–2 tie.

> **Example 2:** Player A sweeps a 5-player table (5 VP + GW). Player E (0 VP) receives a Standings Adjustment → –1 VP. Player A gets 60 TP. The three players tied for 2nd–4th get (48 + 36 + 24) / 3 = 36 TP each. The penalized player, now solely 5th, gets 12 TP.

#### 1.1.4. Disqualification

A **Disqualification (DQ)** is the most serious penalty. It removes the player from the event. Grounds include:

* deliberate cheating and other threats to competitive integrity,
* serious unsportsmanlike conduct, as well as obstructive, abusive, or dangerous behavior.

##### Key Details

* The player loses their current round and is dropped. Prizes are reassigned to the next eligible player; already-distributed participation items (e.g., promo cards) are not reclaimed.
* The disqualified player is removed from standings entirely. Remaining players move up in rank and receive any prizes or qualifications associated with their new placement.
* DQs may also apply to non-players (spectators, staff) compromising the event.
* Proof is not required — the Head Judge need only determine the event's integrity is at risk.
* DQs involving intentional acts compromising integrity or safety must be reported to the **VEKN Ethics Committee**. DQs from accumulated unintentional infractions do not require escalation.

##### Game State Adjustment During an Ongoing Game

A DQ is **not an oust** — no VPs or pool are awarded. The judge must update the game state fairly with minimal disruption:

1. **Remove the player immediately.** Their turn ends; all minions, cards, pool, hand, and materials are removed. Ongoing effects they controlled end unless a card explicitly states otherwise.
2. **No VP or pool awarded.** The predator gains nothing — the DQ is an administrative removal, not a game action.
3. **Adjust seating.** Predator and prey of the removed player become adjacent. Seating-dependent effects (e.g., The Coven) adapt.
4. **Restarting the table** is strongly discouraged — only appropriate during **five-player games** when the DQ occurs very early (before meaningful predator–prey dynamics).
   * **Preliminary restart**: reseated as a 4-player table with fresh decks.
   * **Final restart**: the next highest-ranked player replaces the DQ'd player; the full pre-final seating process is conducted again.
5. **Edge cases.** Benefits from earlier legal interactions with the DQ'd player stand unless corrupted by the infraction. Avoid cascading corrections unless a clear unfair advantage exists. For corrupted game states, the Head Judge may roll back per Section 1.4.
6. **Judge discretion.** The Head Judge may deviate if standard procedure produces a clearly inequitable result. Any deviation must be explained to the table and reported.

### 1.2. Applying Penalties

This guide applies to all sanctioned VTES events (though not all infractions apply to every event). Penalties should be applied as soon as possible, ideally during the same round. Once a tournament is concluded and approved, no retroactive penalties apply except for proven intentional misconduct handled by the VEKN Ethics Committee.

#### 1.2.1. Escalation of Penalties

Repeated same-infraction offenses escalate through penalty levels, each applied twice before moving to the next:

**Caution → Caution → Warning → Warning → Standings Adjustment → Standings Adjustment → Disqualification**

Escalation starts at the infraction's baseline penalty and resets each event. Prior event history does not advance escalation, but previously recorded Warnings remain visible as context. A consistent pattern across events may warrant reclassification under Unsportsmanlike Conduct. Accumulation-based DQs must be from same-event infractions only.

#### 1.2.2. Issuing Significant Penalties

Only the Head Judge should issue Standings Adjustments or Disqualifications (except clear decklist errors). The Head Judge approves any deviation from default procedures. Non-registered individuals receiving such penalties must be temporarily added to the roster with a VEKN number assigned. Penalties remain valid even if the individual drops or withdraws.

#### 1.2.3. Communication and Transparency

When issuing any penalty, the judge must explain: (1) the infraction, (2) the game-state correction, and (3) the penalty and escalation rules. Any deviation from the guide must include both the standard remedy and the justification.

#### 1.2.4. Guidelines for Applying Time Extensions

Time extensions compensate only for time lost due to judge intervention — not normal table talk, slow decisions, or late arrivals. The Head Judge must announce at the start of the tournament whether lost time is handled via clock stops, added extensions, or both.

**Baseline durations:**

* Under 1 minute: no extension normally needed.
* 1–3 minutes: +2 minutes
* 3–5 minutes: +3 to +5 minutes
* Over 5 minutes: +5 to +10 minutes

Never add more time than was actually lost. Extensions stack across multiple interventions during a round, capped at 10 minutes total unless the Head Judge authorizes more.

**Procedure:** Announce the extension to the table, record it (result slip, tracking sheet, or software), and communicate the new ending time.

#### 1.2.5. Consistency and Protection Against Bias

Mandatory remedies exist to shield judges from accusations of bias. Judges should not attempt to "improve" a penalty's effect based on game state, seating, or perceived fairness — even small deviations risk unintentional favoritism. Following written procedures consistently preserves tournament integrity.

#### 1.2.6. Handling Linked Infractions

If a single error triggers multiple related infractions, issue only the most severe one.

#### 1.2.7. Assessing Intent

VTES is a highly political game where suboptimal or counterintuitive plays are often strategically valid. Outcome alone is never sufficient to determine intent.

**Principles:**

* **Assume good faith.** Misconduct must be supported by observable behavior, not inferred from disagreement with strategy.
* **Evaluate patterns, not isolated actions.** Repeated behavior producing the same external benefit is more relevant than a single unusual play.
* **Consider game context.** Pool totals, table position, timing, deck archetype, and remaining time matter.
* **Let players explain.** An explanation need not be correct, only plausible within the game state.

**Key distinctions requiring intent assessment:** Not Playing to Win vs. bad strategy; Stalling vs. slow decision-making; Collusion vs. political cooperation; Cheating vs. procedural error; Aggressive Behaviour vs. emotional frustration; Rage-Quitting vs. forced drop.

**When intent is unclear:** ask neutral questions, observe subsequent behavior, prefer the least severe ruling consistent with the facts, and document the incident. Do not punish poor play, infer intent from table talk alone, or escalate on speculation. Only apply intent-dependent penalties (Standings Adjustments, Disqualifications) when intent is reasonably established.

### 1.3. Randomizing a Library

Before re-randomizing a library, the judge must identify which portion is in a known or arranged order. Consult players and review the ash heap, removed cards, and cards in play that may have influenced library order.

Separate the known portion, shuffle the rest, then return any cards whose positions were legally known or fixed to their appropriate locations. Judge shuffles for rulings do not count as game shuffles and cannot trigger card effects.

### 1.4. Game State Corrections

#### 1.4.1. Rolling Back the Game State

A roll-back reverses the game to the exact moment before an erroneous action — complete and sequential, in strict reverse order. Selective or incomplete roll-backs are never permitted. Only the Head Judge may approve; if seated in the affected game, authority passes to the next available judge.

A roll-back is appropriate only when no player's subsequent decisions were meaningfully shaped by information gained after the error, and the reconstructed sequence would plausibly unfold the same way. Because VTES decision trees branch rapidly, roll-backs should be attempted only with very few subsequent choices or hidden effects.

**Execution**: Reverse each action in reverse order. Cards returned to a hidden area unknown to any player are selected randomly from legal candidates. Cards revealed due to the error are shuffled back into the library — unless already drawn into a hand (their identity is now public). Exercise extreme caution with hidden or random elements, especially shuffle or search effects.

**Simple roll-back**: Reverses only the most recent game action, with no randomization. Used when a small correction cleanly resolves the situation.

**When inappropriate**: If it would require reconstructing branching decisions, heavy randomization, unwinding several turns, or altering decisions based on now-public information, use a Partial Game State Correction instead (Section 1.4.2).

#### 1.4.2. Partial Game State Corrections

A partial correction is used when a full roll-back is infeasible (too much hidden information revealed, too many decisions taken, or too complex to unwind) but leaving the illegal state in place would grant a continuing advantage or distort game integrity. It removes or neutralizes only the illegal elements without reversing past actions.

Principles:

1. **Remove the Illegal Element** — Remove the illegal card, counters, or effect in the least disruptive way. Cards played without meeting requirements typically go to the ash heap. Attribute corrections (blood, life, capacity) may be made only if they don't require unwinding past actions.
2. **Minimize Collateral Changes** — Adjust only what is necessary to restore legality. Do not reconstruct past actions, draws, or mandatory effects.
3. **Accept Past Play As-Is** — Past sequences stand "as played," even if they would have unfolded differently.
4. **Avoid Randomization** — No shuffling or hidden-information adjustments unless the illegal element is in a hidden area and cannot legally remain there.
5. **Head Judge Approval Required** — Partial corrections require Head Judge approval whenever possible.

## 2. Procedural Errors

This category covers mistakes from incorrect execution of game procedures or rules—anything that disrupts proper play but does not fall under cheating or unsportsmanlike conduct. This document cannot list every possible case; judges should use the following guidelines to evaluate procedural mistakes and apply appropriate penalties.

Because deceitful players can exploit procedural errors, this category has a strict escalation policy.

### 2.1. Procedural Error — Missed Mandatory Effect

**Definition:**
A player fails to demonstrate awareness of a mandatory effect generated by a card or game effect under their control before that effect should visibly impact the game.

**Examples:**

1. A player forgets to burn 1 pool for an Anarch Revolt in play.
2. A player controls a ready vampire with no blood, who must hunt as the first action of the turn. Instead, they announce a bleed action with another vampire as their first action.
3. A player plays Charismatic Aura to cancel Immortal Grapple, not realizing that a Dark Influences in play will cancel the Charismatic Aura as it is played.

**Philosophy:**
Players are expected to track their own mandatory effects. Minor missed effects should not generate harsh penalties unless they significantly affect the game state. If the judge believes the player intentionally ignored their own mandatory effect, see [4.7. Unsportsmanlike Conduct – Cheating](#47-unsportsmanlike-conduct--cheating).

**Penalty:**

**Caution**.
Upgraded to a **Warning** if the mandatory effect is detrimental to the controlling player, or missing it creates an advantage.

**Additional Remedy:**
If the effect can still be resolved without major disruption, the judge may instruct the player to apply it immediately. Otherwise, the game continues as-is.

### 2.2. Procedural Error — Card Access Error

**Definition:**
A player gains access to cards they were not entitled to see or draw, or fails to access cards they were required to draw. This includes looking at extra cards, drawing too many or too few cards, or drawing at an incorrect time (including initial draw errors).

Card Access Errors involve only accidental access or missed access to hidden information. If intentional, evaluate under **Unsportsmanlike Conduct — Cheating**.

**Examples:**

1. A player picks up two cards from their library when they were supposed to draw only one.
2. A player accidentally reveals or looks at the top card of their library while shuffling their hand.
3. A player forgets to draw to their full hand size at the end of their minion phase.
4. During setup, a player draws an incorrect number of cards for their initial hand.
5. A player resolves an effect that instructs them to draw a card but fails to do so, only noticing several turns later.
6. A player draws a card before fully announcing its effects.
7. Player A replaces a card after player B asked player A not to because player B wanted time to consider cancelling it.

**Philosophy:**
VTES relies on controlled access to hidden information. Card access errors can distort strategic decisions, alter bluffing dynamics, and create unfair advantages. The judge's goal is to restore fairness while maintaining fluidity of multiplayer play. Most errors are unintentional, but repeated carelessness can harm game integrity and must be tracked.

**Penalty:**

**Caution.**
Formally tracked; signals mishandled hidden information affecting fairness. Intentional misuse must be upgraded to **Unsportsmanlike Conduct — Cheating** (Disqualification).

**Additional Remedy:**

* Reverse or mitigate information gain whenever possible.
* Return extra cards to the appropriate area (library, hand, ash heap) in a fair manner.
  * If identifiable (verified by a judge or witnesses), return those specific cards to the appropriate hidden area and shuffle.
  * If unidentifiable, remove the appropriate number of cards at random from the hidden area (e.g., the hand) and shuffle them into the appropriate hidden area (e.g., the library).
* Complete required draws if the player failed to draw when obligated.
* Reconstruct the game state only insofar as it does not compromise hidden information integrity.

If any fix requires unusual correction, the Head Judge makes the final determination.

### 2.3. Procedural Error — Game Rule Violation

**Definition:**
A player violates the rules of VTES or performs a game procedure incorrectly, when the error is not covered by another specific category.

**Examples:**

1. A player pays an incorrect blood cost for a card.
2. A player plays a legal card (Conditioning) but incorrectly declares its effect, applying the text of a different card (Bonding).
3. A player attempts an action their minion is not allowed to perform.
4. A player plays a master card whose requirements they do not meet (e.g., Art Museum without a ready Toreador).
5. A player attempts to play Parity Shift when there is no legal target available.
6. A player places a card in the wrong area (e.g., Dark Influences in the ash heap instead of in play).

**Philosophy:**
Although attributable to one player, VTES's multiplayer structure means all players must remain attentive to the shared game state. This infraction must be handled consistently regardless of impact.

**Penalty:**

**Caution**, if the violation can be fixed with a simple roll-back or correct declaration of the played card's effect.

**Warning**, if the violation cannot be fixed with a simple roll-back.

**Additional Remedy:**

The judge may:

* Perform a simple roll-back if it would not cause major disruption.
* If the correct card was played but its effect was declared incorrectly, require the player to correctly declare the effect.
* If the player clearly declared a legal card but mistakenly placed a different legal card from their hand, treat the declared card as the card played, provided that:
  * the declared card is legal and was in the player's hand at the time,
  * the declaration unambiguously identifies a single card,
  * no replacement card has been drawn,
  * no hidden information has been revealed,
  * and no player decisions depended on which physical card was played.
* Apply a partial fix if the error fits one of the following:
  * A player failed to unlock (non-infernal) minions during their unlock phase (same turn only).
  * A player made an illegal mandatory choice (e.g., choosing a trait or target).
  * A card is in an incorrect area but easily movable to the correct one.
* Leave the game state as-is if correction would be too disruptive.

The judge should correct the game state with the least disruptive option, following this hierarchy:

1. **Simple Roll-back** — Undo the action to just before the error, provided no major decisions have been made and no hidden information has changed.
2. **Partial Fix** — Apply a targeted correction for basic reversible patterns:
  * A minion failed to unlock during their unlock phase (same turn).
  * An illegal mandatory choice was made (e.g., invalid target).
  * A card is in an incorrect area and can be cleanly moved.
  * A card was illegally played and can be shuffled back into the library if hidden information was revealed.
3. **Structured Forward Fix** — If neither above applies, apply a forward correction that removes any advantage gained: unlock minions improperly locked, restore costs, return resources, or randomize hidden areas as needed.

If opponent(s) could reasonably have noticed the error but failed to, they may also receive a **Procedural Error — Failure to Maintain Game State**.

### 2.4. Procedural Error — Failure to Maintain Game State

**Definition:**
A player notices a visible, relevant, and ongoing Game Rule Violation, reasonably has the opportunity to speak up, but does not do so.

This applies only when the judge determines the player actually noticed the error (through clear evidence, statements, or circumstances). It does not apply when the player was distracted, did not observe the action, or could not reasonably recognize the mistake. It also does not apply to missed mandatory effects, which are handled separately.

**Examples:**

1. A player sees their predator announce a Govern the Unaligned bleed action with an already-locked vampire, but says nothing.
2. A player plays a second master card without an extra master-phase-action–granting effect in play, plainly visible to everyone; another player notices but remains silent.
3. A player notices an acting vampire performs a non-repeatable action twice in the same turn and does not call attention to it.
4. A player realizes another player sent a vampire into torpor without paying the cost of Claws of the Dead but does not mention it.
5. A player transfers more pool than allowed to an uncontrolled vampire; another player sees the incorrect amount but says nothing despite this being a highly visible part of the turn sequence.

**Philosophy:**
All participants share responsibility for maintaining a correct game state. Players are expected to speak up when they recognize a clear and ongoing violation. This penalty reinforces cooperative game-state maintenance, not to punish momentary inattention. Judges should only issue this infraction when it is evident the player noticed the error and chose not to intervene—it is not intended to create cascading penalties for every mistake.

**Penalty:**

**Standings Adjustment**.
Repeated offenses in Procedural Error — Failure to Maintain Game State will never escalate in severity.

## 3. Tournament Errors

### 3.1. Tournament Errors — Deck Problems

Covers deck construction and integrity issues at Constructed and Limited events. Deck checks and decklists ensure decks remain legal and unaltered throughout the tournament.

#### 3.1.1. Deck Problem — Illegal Decklist

**Definition:**
Applies only at tournaments where decklists are required. A decklist is illegal if:

* The list violates card-count requirements (fewer than format minimum, etc.).
* The list includes banned or format-illegal cards.
* The list contains vampires with incorrect crypt grouping.
* The list is unclear or ambiguous in a way that would allow post-submission deck alteration.

This also covers cases where the decklist must be corrected due to unavoidable card loss during the tournament.

**Examples:**

1. A player registers 58 library cards for a Constructed event (minimum is 60).
2. A player lists Madness of the Bard, which is banned.
3. A player writes "Haven" when the format contains multiple cards with that name fragment ("Haven Affinity", "Haven Hunting", "Haven Uncovered").
4. A player lists Arika (a group-2 vampire) and Lutz von Hohenzollern (a group-4 vampire).
5. A player loses some cards mid-event and cannot produce legitimate replacements.

**Philosophy:**
The decklist is the authoritative reference for what the player intends to play. Illegal, unclear, or mismatching lists can provide opportunities for abuse, so judges must ensure lists are accurate and unambiguous. A Standings Adjustment is appropriate only when errors harm tournament integrity or cannot be corrected without disadvantage to opponents.

If a shorthand name unambiguously refers to a specific card, the Head Judge may reduce the penalty. Decklist errors found outside a game are normally applied at the start of the next round, unless the actual deck was itself illegal.

**Penalty:**

**Warning**, unless the main deck and decklist are both illegal and cannot be reconciled without substantially altering the deck; in that case:
**Standings Adjustment**, and the player receives five minutes to adjust the decklist so the physical deck becomes legal.

**Additional Remedies:**

* Remove banned or illegal cards from the decklist.
* Correct unclear entries when their meaning is unambiguous.
* If the player is short of cards, they must add basic, universally available cards (e.g., "Blood Doll", "Wake with Evening's Freshness") to reach the minimum deck size. These substitutions may be reverted later without penalty if originals are found.
* Modify the decklist to match the legal deck the player is actually playing, provided that does not conceal misconduct.
* **For incorrect crypt grouping:**
  * The Head Judge must correct the crypt to a legal configuration. Any substituted vampire must be legal, unambiguous, and no more advantageous than the original. Common neutral substitutions (such as Anarch Convert) may be used only if they do not confer a strategic benefit.
  * If the illegal vampire has already entered play: the judge may replace them with a legal vampire of equal or greater capacity. Excess pool spent is not refunded, and blood may be adjusted to avoid advantage. If a fair correction is not possible, the vampire may be removed from play.
  * All corrections must restore legality while preserving competitive integrity and avoiding retroactive optimization.

#### 3.1.2. Deck Problem — Illegal Main Deck (Legal Decklist)

**Definition:**
This infraction applies only when decklists are in use. A player has an illegal main deck even though the decklist is legal if:

* The deck's card count is illegal (too few or too many cards).
* The deck contains cards illegal for the event.
* The deck contains vampires with incorrect crypt grouping.
* The deck's contents do not match the registered decklist.

This includes misplaced cards from previous games or missing cards.

**Examples:**

1. A player presents 59 library cards while 60 appear on the decklist.
2. A player has "Anthelios: The Red Star" (a banned card) in their deck.
3. A player has both Enkidu (a group-4 vampire) and Beast, The Leatherface of Detroit (a group-2 vampire) in their deck.
4. A card from a previous opponent is accidentally mixed into the deck.
5. An altered card whose artwork is offensive or makes it unrecognizable.

**Philosophy:**
The priority is to restore the deck to match the list. For differences where the listed card has requirements incompatible with the deck crypt but the actual card matches (e.g., "Campground Hunting Ground" listed instead of "Zoo Hunting Ground" with Gangrel-only crypt), the judge corrects the decklist instead.

**Penalty:**

**Standings Adjustment**.
The player must adjust the deck to match the list (or vice versa if the decklist was the error source). All corrections must be complete before the next round.

**Additional Remedies:**

* Retrieve any out-of-place cards (e.g., in other players' decks, on tables).
* Identify all incorrect cards in all areas, reveal them to opponents, and replace with the correct cards.
* If more cards are removed than added, remove non-library cards first; if replacements still exceed removals, replace using random cards from the library.
* If the missing card came from another player's deck, issue penalties to both players.

#### 3.1.3. Deck Problem — Illegal Main Deck (No Decklists Used)

**Definition:**
This applies at events where decklists are not used (e.g., storyline events, small store events). A deck is illegal if:

* The deck's card count is illegal (too few or too many cards).
* The deck contains cards illegal for the event.
* The deck contains vampires with illegal grouping.
* The deck contains improper proxy cards (black and white, unclear printing, etc.) and/or Dark Pack cards.

**Examples:**

* A Constructed deck has only 58 library cards.
* A player includes a banned card such as Madness of the Bard.

**Philosophy:**
Even without decklists, players must begin with a legal deck. Disqualification is not appropriate for unintentional deck errors; judges should correct the deck swiftly and prevent abuse.

**Penalty:**

**Standings Adjustment**.
The player must adjust the deck to make it legal before the next round. Players should be warned before the event about the consequences of presenting an illegal deck.

**Additional Remedies:**

* Remove all illegal cards immediately.
* If the deck is below minimum legal size, add cards using a neutral, non–strategy-altering procedure. Judges must choose one method before making additions and apply it without deviation:
  * Add copies of a neutral filler card (e.g., Burn-Option card or Anarch Convert) until the minimum is reached.
  * Add copies of the card already appearing in the highest quantity in the library, repeating until minimum size.
  * Add cards in descending order of frequency already present (e.g., 8× Govern the Unaligned, then 7× Conditioning, then 6× Deflection — one copy of each in order until the required size is met).
  * **Filler cards must be proxy cards provided by tournament organizers**, only valid for that event, discarded or returned afterward.
  These procedures must be applied strictly without tailoring to the matchup. Never add targeted "counter-cards" that would provide specific strategic advantage.
* If the deck is oversized, remove random cards until it meets all requirements.
* Once corrected, the player continues with a legal deck.
* **For incorrect crypt grouping:**
  * The Head Judge must correct the crypt to a legal configuration. Any substituted vampire must be legal, unambiguous, and no more advantageous than the original. Common neutral substitutions (such as Anarch Convert) may be used only if they do not confer a strategic benefit.
  * If the illegal vampire has already entered play: the judge may replace them with a legal vampire of equal or greater capacity. Excess pool spent is not refunded, and blood may be adjusted to avoid advantage. If a fair correction is not possible, the vampire may be removed from play.
  * All corrections must restore legality while preserving competitive integrity and avoiding retroactive optimization.

### 3.2. Tournament Error — Outside Assistance

**Definition:**
After a game has started, a player, spectator, or participant provides or receives strategic, tactical, or hidden-information input that could influence an ongoing game, or references pre-prepared strategic materials while the round is in progress.

Outside Assistance **does not include**:

* Reviewing one's own decklist during an event.
* Taking notes about the ongoing game (if it does not slow play).
* Discussing finals seating choices before sitting down at the finals table.
* Ordinary table talk among players currently seated at the same table.

Outside Assistance **does include**:

* Receiving or giving strategic/tactical advice from/to anyone not seated in the current game.
* Sharing private or hidden information (hand contents, uncontrolled region, top card, master phase options, etc.).
* Referring during the round to notes, documents, or analyses created before the round began (other than official card text and your own decklist).

These criteria also apply during deck construction or crypt/library review in limited formats. No notes may be taken during a draft. Notes from outside the current round may only be referenced between games.

**Examples:**

1. A player asks a friend standing behind them, "Should I rescue my vampire or let him torpor?"
2. A spectator whispers to a player, "Your prey has a Deflection in hand; don't bleed yet."
3. During a draft, a player secretly writes down which clans they see being passed.
4. A player text-messages a teammate asking what the correct line of play is.
5. A player who has finished their game quietly indicates to a friend at the same table which minion is the biggest threat.

**Philosophy:**
VTES tournaments evaluate each player's own skill and decision-making — not their ability to follow external advice. Any outside strategic input undermines competitive integrity. Minor card annotations clarifying game text are acceptable; detailed strategy notes on cards are not. The Head Judge is the final authority on acceptable markings or notes. Spectators who commit this infraction may be removed from the venue.

**Responsibility:**

* The individual who provides unsolicited assistance is the primary offender.
* A player who solicits, accepts, or knowingly benefits from Outside Assistance is also committing this infraction.
* If both provider and recipient are players, both may be penalized based on intent, impact, and whether the assistance was solicited.
* If a player immediately rejects the assistance and calls a judge, the judge may mitigate or waive their penalty.

**Penalty:**

**Standings Adjustment.** Applied to the offending party or parties as determined above.

### 3.3. Tournament Error — Slow Play

**Definition:**
A player takes significantly more time than reasonably necessary to perform game actions, make decisions, or maintain the natural pace of the table — including delaying actions, reactions, or referendum decisions.

If the judge believes the delay is deliberate to influence the round outcome or protect tournament position, the appropriate infraction is **Unsportsmanlike Conduct — Stalling**.

**Examples:**

1. A player repeatedly rereads cards in another player's ash heap or uncontrolled region without relevant game-state changes.
2. A player excessively hesitates before basic decisions (blocking, transfers, votes) while the table waits.
3. A player takes an unreasonable amount of time to shuffle after an effect requiring it.
4. A player leaves the table without judge approval, causing the table to wait.
5. A player consistently delays announcing action targets or repeatedly asks for clarifications they do not act upon.

**Philosophy:**
Slow decisions disrupt pacing for all players at the table. Players must act within a reasonable timeframe. Judges should begin with a verbal reminder ("Please play at a reasonable pace"). Slow Play penalizes unreasonable delays, not thoughtful decisions.

**Penalty:**

**Caution.**
Judges should avoid using the word "stalling" in verbal reminders — use "Please play faster" or similar.

**Additional Remedy:**
If Slow Play consumed significant round time, the judge may issue a time extension (see 1.2.4).

### 3.4. Tournament Error — Limited Procedure Violation

**Definition:**
A player makes a technical or procedural mistake during a draft — handling boosters, picks, or draft timing incorrectly, without gaining unauthorized information or attempting to cheat. Applies only to draft-based limited formats.

**Examples:**

1. A player passes a booster to the left when that round requires passing to the right.
2. A player takes longer than the allotted time to make their pick.
3. A player places a card on their drafted pile, then tries to take it back and change their pick.
4. A player tilts their head to look toward other players' hands or draft piles (without actually seeing hidden information).

**Philosophy:**
Draft procedures must run smoothly so all players receive the same information and timing. Even small disruptions can compromise fairness for the entire pod. The goal is to maintain order, not to punish honest mistakes. Events may include additional draft instructions; the Head Judge has final authority.

**Penalty:**

**Caution.**
If the error resulted in gaining unintended information, the infraction may instead fall under Card Access Error or Outside Assistance.

**Additional Remedy:**

* Correct the draft flow (direction, timing, or pick order).
* Restore the booster or card to the appropriate place if possible.
* Provide a brief reminder of draft procedures to the pod if needed.
* Apply a time extension (see 1.2.4) only when the intervention meaningfully slowed the pod.

### 3.5. Tournament Error — Public Information Miscommunication

**Definition:**
A player provides incorrect, misleading, or incomplete information about publicly available game elements. Does not apply to general misunderstandings or ambiguous board states.

**Examples:**

1. A player is asked how much pool they have and replies "eleven," but actually has nine.
2. A player asks "How much blood is on your vampire?" and the controller replies "Two" when it actually has three.
3. A player claims their minion has \+1 intercept from a permanent effect when it does not.
4. A player announces a "bleed for 1" with a minion that bleeds for 2 due to a bleed bonus.
5. A player incorrectly states which Methuselah currently has the Edge.

**Philosophy:**
Accurate communication is essential in VTES's multiplayer environment. Many errors are honest mistakes; this infraction maintains clarity and fairness, not to assume malice.

**Penalty:**

**Warning.**
Repeated violations will never escalate, unless they significantly impact gameplay or tournament fairness.

**Additional Remedy:**
If the faulty communication directly led to a specific in-game action (or clear non-action), the judge may roll back to the affected action — not to the moment of incorrect communication. Roll-backs should be used carefully to avoid revealing decision-altering information.

### 3.6. Tournament Error — Obscuring Game State

**Definition:**
A player creates or maintains an ambiguous or misleading physical game layout that obscures public information. This applies only to unclear presentation, not incorrect verbal communication (covered under 3.5).

This includes but is not limited to:

* Overlapping or stacking cards in a way that hides normally visible information.
* Placing cards in non-standard positions that blur the distinction between controlled, uncontrolled, locked, ready, or torpor.
* Failing to clearly separate minions, equipment, and attachments.
* Positioning pool/blood/life counters in confusing ways (e.g., active pool and "blood bank" counters too close together).
* Persistently presenting a disorganized play area after being instructed to clarify it.

**Philosophy:**
VTES relies on publicly visible information. Ambiguous layouts can lead to misunderstandings or unintentional advantage and must be corrected promptly. This infraction does not assume malicious intent.

**Penalty:**

**Caution**.

**Additional Remedies:**
The judge instructs the player to immediately reorganize into a clear, conventional layout. The judge may physically assist (with player consent) to restore clarity.

### 3.7. Tournament Error — Marked Cards

**Definition:**
A player's cards or sleeves show physical inconsistencies that could allow identification of specific cards while in the library, crypt, or hand. This includes scuffs, bends, fading, nail marks, curvature, or differences in card backs, thickness, or sleeves.

If opaque sleeves are used, only sleeves are evaluated. If clear sleeves are used, both sleeves and cards are considered.

**Two severities:**

* **Minor (No Significant Pattern):** Some cards are marked, but not in a way that correlates to card type, clan, discipline, or game relevance.
* **Patterned (Significant Pattern):** Markings correspond clearly to card categories or specific impactful cards, offering meaningful advantage.

**Examples:**

* **Minor:** A few sleeves have small scuff marks across random cards with no correlation. Three cards show corner wear but belong to unrelated types (one master, one action, one action modifier).
* **Patterned:** All reaction cards with wake effects have bent corners. A deck contains Jyhad-backed reaction cards within an otherwise VTES-backed library. All copies of a key card (e.g., Deflection) are in sleeves that curve noticeably more. All titled vampires show consistent right-edge wear.

**Philosophy:**
Cards and sleeves naturally accumulate wear. Without a pattern, the advantage potential is minimal and education plus correction suffices. When markings correlate to card functionality, the advantage becomes substantial and undermines fair play. Markings are assumed unintentional unless strong evidence suggests otherwise.

**Penalty:**

* **Minor / No Significant Pattern:** Warning.
* **Patterned / Significant Pattern:** Standings Adjustment.

**Additional Remedy:**

* The player must replace marked sleeves or cards as soon as possible.
* If clear sleeves are used and cards themselves are worn, the Head Judge may issue proxies.
* If the player lacks replacements, they must re-sleeve the entire deck or obtain sleeves from judge staff.
  * If the deck cannot be brought into a legal, unmarked state within a reasonable timeframe, the player must be removed from the event.
* If the deck must be modified because cards cannot be replaced, the penalty is upgraded to a Standings Adjustment and the decklist updated. This may be reverted if replacements are found later.

### 3.8. Tournament Error — Insufficient Shuffling

**Definition:**
A player unintentionally fails to sufficiently shuffle their library or crypt before presenting it for additional randomization. A deck is not considered shuffled if the judge believes the player could know the position or distribution of one or more cards. This includes forgetting to shuffle after an effect that requires it, or using procedures that do not adequately randomize.

**Examples:**

1. A player searches their library (e.g., Magic of the Smith) and forgets to shuffle afterwards.
2. A player performs only a single riffle shuffle after a library search before offering it to be cut.
3. A player pile-shuffles mid-game in a patterned manner without following with sufficient randomization.

**Philosophy:**
Players are expected to apply proper randomization whenever required. Because opponents may cut or shuffle the deck afterwards, the advantage potential is reduced if procedure is followed. However, any time cards become visible during manipulation or searching, the deck is no longer randomized.

Patterned pile-shuffling is permitted only at the start of the game. Stacking or weaving is acceptable only if followed by thorough randomization. Judges should educate players, as improper shuffling often results from misunderstanding. If intentional deck manipulation is suspected, upgrade to **Unsportsmanlike Conduct — Cheating**.

**Penalty:**

**Warning**.

**Additional Remedy:**
Thoroughly shuffle the affected portion. If only a subset became unrandomized, only that portion needs shuffling, but the judge may require a full shuffle for game integrity.

## 4. Unsportsmanlike Conduct

### 4.1. Unsportsmanlike Conduct — Minor

**Definition:**
Behavior that is disruptive, disrespectful, or emotionally charged, but does not target a specific individual with harassment, intimidation, or coercion. Typically involves brief lapses in etiquette (raised voices, rude remarks, emotional outbursts) that negatively impact the table environment without creating a hostile or unsafe atmosphere. The behavior is non-targeted, situational, and easily correctable.

**Examples:**

1. A player speaks in an overly loud or hostile tone during a rules discussion, causing discomfort but not directing personal insults at anyone.
2. A player uses indecent tournament items (playmats or sleeves with explicit nudity or inflammatory imagery).
3. A player improperly demands that a judge issue a penalty to another participant.
4. A player appeals directly to the Head Judge without allowing the floor judge to rule first.
5. A player reacts to an in-game setback by slamming a hand on the table or muttering angrily, without targeting another player.
6. A player reacts disrespectfully to another player's decisions (eye-rolling, sarcastic remarks), creating an unpleasant environment without directing hostility at someone personally.
7. A player briefly adopts an intimidating posture (leaning forward abruptly, pointing aggressively) but the behavior does not persist or target a specific opponent.
8. A player leaves significant trash or personal items in the play area after leaving the table.
9. A player fails to follow a tournament official's instruction to leave or step away.
10. A player repeatedly interrupts negotiations between other players without being involved.
11. A player slams their cards and says loudly: "This table is ridiculous\! Nothing is going my way today\!"
12. An altered card contains offensive, inappropriate, or disruptive content.

**Philosophy:**
All participants are entitled to a safe, respectful, and enjoyable environment. Conduct crossing acceptable boundaries must be addressed so the tournament environment can be maintained.

**Penalty:**

**Warning**.

**Additional Remedy:**
The player must immediately correct the disruptive behavior.

### 4.2. Unsportsmanlike Conduct — Major

**Definition:**
Targeted, aggressive, or coercive actions directed at another player, judge, or participant that create a hostile, unsafe, or intimidating environment. This includes persistent verbal aggression, personal insults (related to race, color, religion, national origin, age, gender, disability, sexual orientation, etc.), intimidation during negotiations, or any conduct that reasonably makes another participant feel harassed, threatened, or singled out.

The focus is on behavior that becomes personal, pressuring, or humiliating — impact takes precedence over intent. Threats or attempts at physical violence must be treated as **Unsportsmanlike Conduct — Aggressive Behavior**.

**Examples:**

1. A player uses a racial or discriminatory slur toward another participant.
2. A player deliberately uses threatening body language or confrontational tone aimed at a specific opponent to pressure them into a deal.
3. A player directs demeaning comments at another participant about their decisions, play skill, or personal traits to influence gameplay or undermine them emotionally.
4. A player repeatedly directs anger at the same person, escalating into a sustained personal attack.
5. A player attempts to force compliance by behaving aggressively (leaning in, menacing tone, threatening gestures), making the targeted person feel unsafe.
6. A player repeatedly misgenders another player or spectator.
7. A player takes photos or videos of another participant without their explicit permission.
8. A player pursues unwanted personal interactions (such as romantic advances).
9. A player intentionally blocks another participant's movement, attempting to provoke physical contact.
10. A spectator uses social media or event-connected channels to mock, threaten, or harass a participant.

**Philosophy:**
Harassment — verbal, digital, or physical — undermines both safety and event integrity. Judges must intervene immediately: stop the match, separate individuals, remove the offender from the immediate area, and educate them regardless of intent. If investigation shows the threshold for Major is not met, judges should still talk with involved parties to prevent further issues.

**Penalty:**

**Standings Adjustment**.

**Additional Remedy:**
The player must correct the behavior immediately, and a written report should be submitted to the VEKN Ethics Committee.

**Upgrade:**
If committed with malicious intent, the player shows no remorse, or the offense recurs, the penalty is **Disqualification** and removal from the venue, reported to the VEKN Ethics Committee.

### 4.3. Unsportsmanlike Conduct — Aggressive Behaviour

**Definition:**
Behavior that is physically threatening, violent, or intentionally damaging toward another person, their belongings, or tournament property — any action that could reasonably make someone fear for their safety or that constitutes physical intimidation.

**Examples:**

1. A player threatens physical harm to another player who refuses to support a deal or concede.
2. A player forcibly pulls a chair away from another participant, causing them to fall.
3. A player verbally threatens a judge after an unfavorable ruling.
4. A player destroys or attempts to destroy another player's cards or materials.
5. A player intentionally flips or strikes a game table.
6. A spectator aggressively confronts a player in a way that causes fear of physical escalation.

**Philosophy:**
Physical intimidation, violence, or threats are never tolerated. This infraction requires immediate removal to protect the tournament community.

**Penalty:**

**Disqualification**.

**Additional Remedy:**

* The organizer must instruct the offender to leave the venue immediately. Reporting to the VEKN Ethics Committee may be necessary depending on severity.
* **Table-Flipping:**
  * Immediately **halt the game**, ensure safety, and secure the area.
  * If possible, **reconstruct the game state** using reliable information (visible cards, pool totals, counters, unanimous player agreement). Reconstruction is only acceptable with high confidence and minimal dispute.
  * If reliably reconstructed, the game may resume with a time extension. If not, the judge must terminate the game.
  * Judges should avoid penalizing remaining players — this may include time extensions, seating adjustments, or tournament-level remedies for irrecoverable interruptions.
  * The incident and all remedial actions must be documented in the tournament report.

### 4.4. Unsportsmanlike Conduct — Bribery and Wagering

**Definition:**
A player offers, requests, hints at, or accepts any out-of-game incentive intended to influence the outcome of a game — such as encouraging a concession, altering play, agreeing to an oust order, or manipulating VP distribution. This includes trading prizes, cards, favors, or other benefits for a specific result.

Wagering occurs when any player or spectator places, accepts, or offers a bet on a tournament outcome, round, or portion of play. The wager need not be monetary, and it does not matter whether the bettor is involved in that game.

**Examples:**

1. A player offers their predator a cash payment to concede so they can make the finals.
2. A player offers a rare VTES card to their prey for an unopposed oust.
3. A player suggests they will concede "if you give me part of your prize support."
4. Two players agree that the winner will receive a card or booster pack from the loser.
5. Two spectators wager on how many VPs a particular player will score.
6. A player negotiates an intentional draw in exchange for non-game benefits.

**Philosophy:**
Negotiation about in-game actions is fundamental to VTES, but negotiation about match results, concessions, or prize outcomes is strictly prohibited. Any attempt to influence results through incentives or outside agreements must be penalized immediately.

**Penalty:**

**Disqualification**. A written report should be submitted to the VEKN Ethics Committee.

### 4.5. Unsportsmanlike Conduct — Theft of Tournament Material

**Definition:**
A player takes, conceals, or intentionally retains materials that do not belong to them — including cards, tokens, counters, seating materials, or equipment provided by the event or belonging to other participants.

**Examples:**

1. A player removes cards from another player's crypt or library between rounds.
2. A player takes a table number or other tournament property.
3. A player notices they have an opponent's card after the round and hides it rather than informing a judge.
4. A player pockets communal components (blood/pool counters) supplied by the organizer.

**Philosophy:**
Participants must trust that their cards and materials remain secure. Theft erodes tournament integrity and is completely unacceptable. Theft of non-tournament personal property is primarily the Tournament Organizer's responsibility, though judges should assist when appropriate.

**Penalty:**

**Disqualification**.

**Additional Remedy:**
The organizer must require the offender to leave the venue immediately. Reporting to the VEKN Ethics Committee may be warranted depending on severity.

### 4.6. Unsportsmanlike Conduct — Stalling

**Definition:**
A player intentionally delays game progress — through inaction, excessively slow decisions, or unnecessary procedural delays — to take advantage of the round time limit. If not intentional, refer to **Tournament Error — Slow Play**.

**Examples:**

1. A player in a favorable position repeatedly "thinks" for long periods during trivial decisions to prevent opponents from responding before time expires.
2. A player leading in VPs deliberately slows their influence or minion phase to prevent others from catching up.
3. A player close to being ousted intentionally prolongs decisions to run down the clock.
4. A player takes excessive time resolving mandatory effects without any purpose other than consuming time.
5. A player performs all steps explicitly in the last few minutes when they used shortcuts earlier (e.g., "ready for strikes" becomes "nothing before range, waiting on response, no maneuver, waiting on response...").

**Philosophy:**
Stalling attacks the fundamental fairness of VTES. Intentionally manipulating pace of play is considered cheating. The key indicator is **behavior change** — especially when in an advantageous position or near the end. A player consistent in their pace throughout should not be forced to alter it. Any deliberate effort to exploit the clock is intolerable.

Stalling is always treated as intentional. Judges must investigate promptly when suspicious patterns occur, especially in timed rounds or finals.

**Penalty:**

**Disqualification**.

**Additional Remedy:**
The player must leave the venue if instructed. Judges should document the incident thoroughly.

### 4.7. Unsportsmanlike Conduct — Cheating

**Definition:**
A person intentionally violates the rules or tournament policies, lies to a tournament official, or becomes aware of a significant offense in their game and chooses not to report it.

For an action to be Cheating, **both** must be true:

* The player is attempting to gain an advantage.
* The player is aware that their action is illegal.

If either criterion is not met, handle as another infraction (Procedural Error, Tournament Error, etc.). Cheating may initially resemble a simple mistake; judges must investigate intent and awareness before determining the infraction.

**Examples:**

1. A player intentionally alters results on a game report without other players knowing.
2. A player lies to a judge about the game state or action order to influence a ruling.
3. A player knowingly allows an opponent to resolve an action incorrectly in a way that benefits them.
4. A player notices an opponent failed to apply a mandatory effect and intentionally stays silent.
5. A player secretly looks at another player's crypt or library during setup or shuffling.
6. A player adds cards not part of their registered decklist.
7. A player realizes they drew an extra card or manipulated card order and chooses not to call a judge.

**Philosophy:**
VTES relies on negotiation, trust, and shared game-state information — intentional deception outside permitted gameplay boundaries is especially harmful. Players who knowingly break rules for personal advantage undermine the entire event. Judges must carefully separate genuine mistakes from intentional misconduct. Once intent and awareness are established, the penalty is mandatory.

**Penalty:**

**Disqualification**.

**Additional Remedy:**
The offender should be removed from the venue at the organizer's discretion. A written report should be submitted to the VEKN Ethics Committee.

### 4.8. Unsportsmanlike Conduct — Fraud

**Definition:**
A player intentionally misrepresents rules, procedures, personal information, or any other tournament-relevant information.

**Examples:**

1. A player registers using a false name or incorrect VEKN ID.
2. A player falsely claims a commonly played card works differently, hoping opponents won't check.
3. A player knowingly explains a tournament procedure incorrectly (seating rules, tiebreakers) to gain an advantage.
4. A player pretends to misunderstand the game state to justify a planned illegal action.
5. A player hides additional boosters received at a limited event and secretly adds them to their deck.
6. A player forges or alters information on an Archon sheet (VP totals, standings).
7. A player intentionally misreports a game result to improve their standings or hurt another player.

**Philosophy:**
Fraud undermines tournament integrity and community trust. Zero tolerance.

**Penalty:**

**Disqualification**. A written report should be submitted to the VEKN Ethics Committee.

### 4.9. Unsportsmanlike Conduct — Collusion

**Definition:**
Players deliberately agree to alter, predetermine, or illegally establish the outcome of a game or match — including any agreement to manipulate standings, table results, or tournament progression outside normal permitted negotiation.

**Examples:**

1. A player agrees to let a friend oust them without resistance to improve the friend's finals chances.
2. Two remaining players agree to flip a coin to decide who concedes the Game Win.
3. Two players at different tables coordinate concessions to influence finals advancement.
4. Players privately agree before a round on who will "take the table" regardless of in-game dynamics.

**Philosophy:**
Table talk and in-game deals are expected, provided they follow strict limitations. A deal is not Collusion as long as:

* No out-of-game incentives are offered (money, cards, favors, etc.).
* Everything is discussed openly at the table — no secret arrangements or off-table conversations.
* No random method (coin flip, die roll, etc.) is used to determine a winner or concession.
* The agreement does not predetermine tournament outcomes beyond what normal legal play allows.

Violating any of these conditions makes the agreement Collusion.

**Penalty:**

**Disqualification**. A written report should be submitted to the VEKN Ethics Committee.

### 4.10. Unsportsmanlike Conduct — Health & Safety Disruption

**Definition:**
A player's physical condition or behavior creates an unsafe, unsanitary, or severely uncomfortable environment for other participants, or prevents them from reasonably participating. This includes extreme hygiene issues, visible intoxication or substance impairment, or any behavior posing a health or safety risk.

**Philosophy:**
Situations that materially affect other players' ability to participate must be addressed promptly and discreetly. The goal is correction, not punishment; judges should handle these cases with professionalism and confidentiality.

**Penalty:**

**Warning → Removal from the venue (if uncorrected).**
The judge should first give a private request to correct the issue. If it cannot be corrected quickly or is severe, the player is removed with no additional penalties.

**Additional Notes:**

* Judges must treat these cases respectfully and avoid shaming language.
* Medical conditions must be handled sensitively; default to accommodation if in doubt.
* Alcohol and substance policies must follow the venue's legal rules.

### 4.11. Unsportsmanlike Conduct — Rage-Quitting

**Definition:**
A player intentionally abandons an ongoing game in anger or frustration — by leaving the table, refusing to continue play, or making it impossible for the game to proceed — without judge authorization and outside of legitimate procedures (e.g., medical emergency).

**Examples:**

1. A player packs their cards and leaves the table mid-game after an unfavorable outcome.
2. A player verbally refuses to take further game actions and ignores judge instructions to continue.
3. A player departs the venue without notifying a judge, causing the table to halt.
4. A player scoops their cards and walks away as a reaction to being targeted, before the game state can be resolved.

**Philosophy:**
All players must remain engaged until the game ends through normal ousting or judge-approved resolutions. Rage-Quitting undermines competitive integrity, disrupts other players, and unfairly affects scoring and standings.

**Penalty:**

**Disqualification**.

**Additional Remedies:**

* The player is immediately removed from the game and the event.
* The judge must update the game state fairly and minimally, treating the player as no longer present.
* A time extension may be issued if the disruption consumed significant round time.
* Additional sanctions may be considered if the behavior is aggressive or disruptive.

### 4.12. Unsportsmanlike Conduct — Failure to Play to Win

**Definition:**
A player intentionally plays in a way that does not pursue their own best possible game result, distorting the natural outcome. Each player is expected to maximize their own success; intentionally abandoning this objective undermines competitive integrity.

Playing to win means, in descending priority:

* Attempt to win the table (game win) by achieving a strict majority of VPs (at least 2 VP, more than any other player).
* If a game win is no longer realistically achievable, attempt to gain as many VPs as possible.
* If a full VP is unreachable, attempt to secure a half VP by surviving until time.

Players are not required to succeed, but must genuinely pursue these goals. Suboptimal play, miscalculation, or incorrect game assessment do not constitute this infraction.

Negotiation, table talk, and deal-making are permitted and unrestricted. A disadvantageous deal is not, by itself, an infraction. Failure to Play to Win occurs only if a player knowingly executes a deal that does not improve, at the time it was made, their chances of winning or maximizing VPs.

**Examples:**

* A player refuses to take an obvious oust that would give them the game win, to favor or punish another player.
* A player with a viable scoring path makes plays that knowingly reduce their own chances without plausible justification.
* A player who can no longer win the table deliberately accelerates their own elimination rather than surviving for a half VP near time-out.
* A player openly states or demonstrates they are "kingmaking," protesting, or acting out of frustration rather than pursuing their own result.
* A player agrees to and fulfills a deal that, at the time, does not improve their chances compared to other available lines of play.
* A player is down to no minion, 3 pool and cannot reach timeout with 30 min left. They are allowed to do 3 transfers to self-oust.
* A player already has the game win with 3VP, their opponent is at 0. They are allowed to concede the rest for a 3-2 victory.
* A player with 2VP is in duel with a player with 1VP. They are allowed to withdraw (giving them the game win at 2.5 to 2); their opponent is allowed to let them if they can't oust to win (1.5 at timeout is an inferior result).

These examples assume intent. Suboptimal play due to poor skill or miscalculation does not constitute this infraction.

**Philosophy:**
Individual decisions cascade to all players. Failing to play to win artificially alters incentives and can invalidate competitive outcomes. Negotiation and alliances must be tools in service of one's own success — not replacements for it.

Judges must evaluate deals based on the player's intent and reasonable perception of the game state at the time. A deal is legal as long as accepting it could reasonably be understood as improving the player's chances. Judges must exercise caution: disagreement with a line of play, hindsight evaluation, or unconventional strategy is not sufficient. This infraction applies only when intent to abandon competitive goals is clear and demonstrable.

**Penalty:**

**Warning**.

In particularly clear or disruptive cases — explicit statements, deliberate game throwing, or repeated behavior — a **Standings Adjustment** may be appropriate.

## 5. Event Organization

This section covers organizational issues relating to the practical functions of running a tournament.

### 5.1. Event Organization — Unexpected Drop

**Description:**
A player leaves the tournament unexpectedly and can no longer continue.

**Resolution:**

* Drop during seating for a 5-player table: convert to a 4-player table.
* Drop during seating for a 4-player table: re-seat players to form legal table sizes (4 or 5), per V:EKN Tournament Rules, Section 3.1.2.
* At large events not using the multi-judge system, if a player drops at a 4-player table after other tables have begun, a tournament official takes the vacant seat as proxy with a randomly selected staff deck.
* If the drop prevents forming legal tables: the event immediately becomes unsanctioned. Between rounds, adjustments may be made (e.g., judge joining as player) to restore sanctioning.
* Drop before round 1 with no legal tables possible: event is unsanctioned; no official prize support distributed.
* Event becomes unsanctioned due to mid-tournament drops: prize support may still be distributed normally. Submit the report as usual and note what happened. The event appears unsanctioned and won't affect ratings.

### 5.2. Event Organization — Venue Interference

**Description:**
The venue imposes constraints requiring round time changes.

**Resolution:**

* Adjust round times as needed.
* The event becomes unsanctioned if any round uses a time limit shorter than 2 hours **and** at least one table ends due to time being called. If no table reaches the time limit, the event remains sanctioned.
* Note venue problems in the event report. Prize support may be distributed normally as long as the event completes.

### 5.3. Event Organization — Force Majeure and Emergency Termination

**Definition:**
Any unexpected external event that makes it unsafe or impossible to continue a sanctioned tournament: fire alarms, power failures, severe weather, disruptive venue staff or third parties, or any circumstance compromising participant safety or event integrity.

**Resolution:**
The Head Judge must immediately halt all games and ensure safety. Once secured, determine whether continuation is possible.

**A. Tournament Cannot Continue**

1. **Safety First** — No further play if the venue is unsafe. Direct players to leave calmly.
2. **Standings** — If the tournament cannot resume within a reasonable timeframe:
  * All in-progress games end immediately with no further VPs awarded.
  * Players keep VPs and GWs from the last fully completed round.
  * Partial rounds do not count, even if some tables finished.
  * If terminated during the final, no winner is awarded unless tournament policy requires otherwise.
3. **Prizes and Qualifications** — Distributed based on the last fully completed round. If no rounds completed, the event is void. Qualifications follow the same principle.
4. **Reporting** — The Head Judge must submit a full written report to VEKN: nature of the event, decision-making process, standings at termination, and any safety issues.

**B. Tournament Can Safely Resume**

* The Head Judge may restart the round (recommended), or resume the current round only if all tables unanimously agree and no competitive information was compromised.
* If any table's game state is corrupted or safety remains uncertain, restart or terminate.

**C. Hostile or Obstructive Venue Staff**

* Treat as Force Majeure. If the venue becomes unsafe, stop the event and direct players to leave.
* Relocation is only allowed if every player can reasonably relocate, no game information was compromised, and the new space meets minimum standards.
* If relocation is not possible, terminate and score as in Section A.

### 5.4. Event Organization — Organizer's / Judge's Recommended Supplies

Organizers and judges should carry standardized supplies to resolve common issues quickly and with minimal disruption.

**Sleeves** — At least enough identical, opaque sleeves for two full decks (library + crypt). Neutral color, suitable for competitive play. Used for marked-card issues or emergency re-sleeving.

**Replacement / Proxy Crypt Cards** — A small pool of universally reasonable replacement vampires (e.g., Anarch Convert, group-appropriate low-impact vampires), clearly marked as judge-issued proxies. Used for illegal crypt groupings or lost/damaged cards. Prioritize neutrality over optimization.

**Spare Library Cards** — Innocuous, universally available cards for reaching minimum deck size: basic masters (Blood Doll), simple reactions (Wake with Evening's Freshness), burn-option or placeholder cards. Temporary substitutions, revertible if originals are recovered.

**Basic Tournament Materials** — Pens, paper, spare blank decklist forms. Printed copies (or offline access) of tournament rules, this guide, and current ban/errata list.

**Timing and Administration Tools** — Stopwatch or timer for time extensions, Slow Play observations, and investigations. Access to the tournament management system (e.g., Archon) where possible.

**Game Aids** — Generic blood/pool counters. Neutral tokens or markers for temporary game-state clarification.
